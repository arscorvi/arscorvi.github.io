Various book recommendations.

THE SON OF MAN
by Jean-Baptiste Del Amo

An easy pick for one of my favourite books of all time. A veritable masterclass in writing scenery, wherein the author describes environments (particularly wilderness and wildlife) with such vivid detail that I may as well have been living in the mountains. Sprinkle in the fact that there’s little dialogue, a minimal cast of characters — none of which are named — and the overarching themes of family legacies, generational trauma and fatherhood, and you get an incredible book.

NO LONGER HUMAN
by Osamu Dazai

One of the most popular and notable examples of the Japanese I-novel: works of fiction that correspond, either literally or figuratively, to events in the author’s life. Incredibly bleak and depressing, my desire to shake the main character by the shoulders whilst reading this was strong, but this was, for the longest time, the first book that came to mind when asked what my favourite piece of literature was. An iconic work of fiction that was pivotal to the development of my tastes in literature.

THE FLOWERS OF BUFFOONERY
by Osamu Dazai

Follows the same titular character from the above, and I thoroughly recommend reading this immediately after finishing No Longer Human. It’s absurd, it’s light-hearted, and it tempers the bleakness of the characters’ lives by dulling the sharpness and stress and instilling them with a more easily recognisable humanity. Nothing matters, everything is weird, and we’re all buffoons.

DEADKIDSONGS
by Toby Litt

A hard book to recommend because of the sheer amount of violence, but I’m recommending it anyway; the way that the author captures the ferocity and tribalism of British youth is rivalled only by works such as Lord of the Flies. High-stakes, absurd, and uncomfortable in parts. Full of enough twists to keep me reading into the early hours, and I adore potentially unreliable narrators as a literary device.

SATANTANGO
by László Krasznahorkai

An incredible book about the cyclical nature of our lives, what it means to be part of a community, and just how far we’ll go to improve our lot. There are enough characters here, each with their own distinct and expertly captured personality, to build a strong sense of realism. Difficult to read in parts (the author isn’t a big fan of paragraphs, so it’s wall after wall of text), but it’s well worth the effort; in particular, the dream-like sequence in the latter half of the book cemented this as one of my favourites.

A DEATH IN THE FAMILY
by Karl Ove Knausgård

This one flew under my radar for far longer than it deserved. Granted, people either love Knausgård or loathe him, and I’m not one to typically buy into the hyperbole that is descriptors like ‘The International Phenomenon’, but it’s a relatively apt phrase in this case. Part memoir, part biography, and part fiction, it throws the reader, unapologetically, into the life of the author and the whirlwind of emotions surrounding the death of his father. There are certainly parts that drag, and some sentences are strung together haphazardly and given no further thought, but it’s a unique reading experience.

CHILDISH LITERATURE
by Alejandro Zambra

This whole book is delightful, uplifting, and spirited. From start to finish, there are countless musings on writing, on literature, on our relationship with the written word — from our very first books to what we read as adults — and the budding development of our authorial voices. It’s written from the perspective of parenthood, but it naturally delves into the topic of childhood in equal measure, since you can’t quite have one without the other. One chapter in particular — The Kid with No Dad — is a standout piece of storytelling.

CONFESSIONS OF A MASK
by Yukio Mishima

Another iconic work of fiction by one of Japan’s most celebrated authors. There remains a lot of debate as to whether or not this book is an I-novel or otherwise autobiographical in nature, but as a standalone piece of literature, it’s powerful enough to deserve the high praise it so often gets. First published in 1949, it serves as a snapshot of queer life in pre- and post-war Japan; the relentless interpersonal struggle, the sense of not being ’normal’, and of having to wear the titular mask in order to hold any kind of merit and meaning in society.

THE SAILOR WHO FELL FROM GRACE WITH THE SEA
by Yukio Mishima

Obsession: the book. A wonderful exercise in writing about the ugliness that can come with blind admiration without having to overtly point out the fact that the main character is, in fact, obsessive. Violent in parts, poetic in others. Like the majority of Mishima’s work, it delves into themes of belonging, purpose, and the constant dance we play with society as a whole.
