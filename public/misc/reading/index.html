Reading for the Sake of Writing

I don’t read as much as I should. I have stacks upon stacks of books — spilling out of a bookshelf and across my desk, my coffee table, my other shelves — that are, for the most part, unread. Like many readers, I have a peculiar habit of frequently adding to my library and never quite catching up with it, and the time that I actually set aside for reading is becoming less and less.

Nonetheless, it remains an important and pivotal part of my life; I have an entire section dedicated to literature on this site for a reason. And the more time I spend thinking about how my reading habits have developed over the years, the easier it is for me to identify a few key habits, those preferences and mannerisms that make up the peculiar structure that is how I read. The easier it is, too, for me to identify just how much various authors and works of literature have influenced my own writing.

As an example: when I was in my mid teens, I picked up a copy of Gardens of the Moon — the first book in Steven Erikson’s tremendous Malazan: Book of the Fallen series — and promptly devoured it as quickly as I could. I had been writing (predominantly through the medium of role-play) for years, and this book was eye-opening and fascinating to me; the characters had so much depth, the world and its nature were so alive, and even the most mundane details, such as dust being kicked up by a horse’s hoof, were written in such a way as to spark a whirlwind of strong emotions.

The most prevalent of which was jealousy; my own writing was nothing quite like this. I was never that good, even if I was getting praise from my peers and enjoying some semblance of popularity in the social circles I was a part of; to a teenager, those things are overwhelmingly important, but there I was, being confronted with what seemed at the time to be an unattainable height, a level of commanding words that I could never reach, and it upset me greatly. Why couldn’t I write like that? What was I missing? Surely, if I just read more, I would be able to figure it out?

So I read. I don’t intend to go into an awful lot of detail about the tremendously bloated pastiche that developed as a result of this habit, but the pastiche itself was — and still is — fascinating to me. I was no longer reading exclusively for the sake of enjoyment or escapism, but more so for the sake of bettering my own writing and, by extension, myself. I engaged with literature in a wholly new light. Books were suddenly the most important thing in the whole entire world, and there was no greater ideal to strive for than to be a reader. Writing, on the other hand, could come later; it became something to enjoy only once I had read enough, learned enough, and grown enough.

Unsurprisingly, during those years, I never wrote anything at all.

I was, in a way, reading for the sake of writing. To this day, I can pick a book from my library and give two entirely different recommendations; one for what I like about the story itself and one exclusively for what it taught me about writing. Have I actually done anything with all of this learning and knowledge? No. But it did, in a roundabout way, help me to broaden my literary horizons, discover new authors, and experience new perspectives and cultures that I would have otherwise never discovered.

It’s hard to say whether or not this was a healthy way to form a habit of reading, even if it has given me the opportunity to think back and reflect on why reading is so important to me. As I’ve gotten older, what I think and what I feel about reading has become much harder to vocalise, but the following quote — from Alejandro Zambra’s remarkable book, Childish Literature — cuts through most of the chaff:

Reading to yourself: it’s odd that we use this metaphorical expression to allude to silent reading. In reality there’s no such thing as silent reading: reading has a built-in voice included in the seeming silence, a voice that the silence can’t destroy. For now, your reading to yourself is audible. And that murmur, that stammer, that joyful sounding out — sometimes it sounds like a secret. Of course it does: to read is to receive secrets, but also to tell secrets to oneself.

The irony of using someone else’s words to describe my own thoughts and feelings is not lost on me; I’m still re-learning, slowly but surely, how to read for the sake of reading.
